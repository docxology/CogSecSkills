# Devil's Advocacy

Devil's Advocacy is a structured challenge-analysis technique from Heuer &
Pherson. An analyst is deliberately assigned to argue the most credible case
AGAINST a prevailing consensus or lead judgment — not to be contrarian, but to
test whether the consensus holds up when a smart, honest adversary attacks it.
The result is either a strengthened consensus that survived real challenge, or
an early warning that the lead judgment rests on softer ground than it appeared.

## When to use

- A consensus or lead judgment has formed quickly, with little dissent, and you
  want to know whether it is genuinely robust or merely comfortable.
- A high-stakes judgment rests on a small number of key assumptions that have
  not themselves been challenged.
- Group dynamics, hierarchy, or momentum may be suppressing legitimate
  alternative interpretations.
- You need to surface what new collection or evidence would actually resolve a
  contested judgment.

## What it produces

- A restatement of the consensus and the explicit assumptions and evidence it
  rests on.
- The strongest good-faith counter-case a capable adversary-analyst would mount,
  with its supporting evidence and reasoning marshalled honestly.
- A robustness verdict: did the consensus survive the challenge, partially
  survive, or fail — and the new collection that would resolve the dispute.

## Procedure

The full step-by-step procedure, with tool verbs and anti-criteria, lives in
[workflow.md](workflow.md). In brief: state the consensus and its load-bearing
assumptions, select the assumptions most vulnerable to challenge, review the
evidence base for contradicting or merely-assumed items, construct the most
credible alternative interpretation in good faith, marshal the counter-case
honestly, and then assess robustness and resolving collection.

## Key discipline

The advocate argues the contrary case in good faith — this is a role assigned to
test robustness, not a license for contrarianism. Build the strongest real
counter-case, never a straw man. Distinguish evidence that genuinely contradicts
the consensus from evidence that is merely assumed or absent. And if the
consensus fails the challenge, report that result plainly — never suppress an
inconvenient outcome to protect the prevailing view.
