# Structured Literature Synthesis

## When to use

Use this skill whenever you have several sources — papers, reports, articles,
threads, notes — and need a single trustworthy account of what they collectively
say. It is the right tool when the question is "what does the research say?" and
the honest answer requires weighing agreement against disagreement rather than
quoting whichever source is loudest. Reach for it before building any
downstream cognitive-security analysis that depends on a defensible reading of a
literature.

## What it produces

- A **BLUF synthesis briefing**: bottom line up front, then themes with graded
  evidence, conflicts surfaced honestly, gaps with what would resolve them, and
  full citations.
- An **evidence table**: every extracted claim mapped to its source citation and
  a quality grade, so each synthesized statement is traceable.
- An explicit **gap inventory**: questions no source in the corpus answers.

## Procedure

The full step-by-step method, with tool verbs and the evidence-strength rubric,
lives in [workflow.md](workflow.md). In summary: define the question and
inclusion/exclusion criteria; gather and deduplicate sources; extract graded
claims with citations from each; cluster findings by theme; identify agreements,
conflicts, and gaps; grade the strength of evidence per theme; and write the
BLUF synthesis.

## Key discipline

- **Traceability.** Every synthesized statement maps back to specific sources.
  No claim appears in the briefing without a citation behind it.
- **Honest conflict.** When sources disagree, the disagreement is reported as a
  conflict — never silently resolved in favor of one side or smoothed into a
  false consensus.
- **Evidence grading.** Each theme carries an explicit strength grade
  (strong / moderate / weak / insufficient) reflecting how much and how good the
  supporting evidence is. A single source's claim is never presented as settled
  consensus, and the synthesis never asserts more than the sources support.
