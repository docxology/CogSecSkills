# Issue Redefinition

Issue Redefinition is a structured creativity and reframing technique that systematically restates an analytic question in multiple alternative forms to expose assumptions embedded in the original framing and reveal new solution paths. By articulating five or more distinct restatements of the same underlying problem, analysts often discover that the initial question was too narrow, too broad, or subtly loaded in ways that constrain good analysis. The technique draws on lateral thinking and problem-structuring research to break the anchoring effect of the initial formulation.

## When to use

- at the start of an analytic task when the original question feels constraining or produces an analysis that goes nowhere
- when an analytic team has been working on a problem for a long time and may be anchored on an inherited framing
- when a consumer's question appears to embed a flawed assumption that would lead the analysis astray
- when an analysis of competing hypotheses generates only uninformative or trivially similar hypotheses — a sign that the framing is too narrow

## What it produces

- five or more alternative restatements of the original question using different framing levers: broadening, narrowing, inverting, shifting actors, shifting time horizon, changing the unit of analysis
- for each restatement: the assumption removed or added, the solution space opened or closed
- a recommended preferred framing with explicit rationale
- a documented record of rejected framings and why they were set aside

## Key discipline

- generate at least five restatements before evaluating any — premature evaluation kills the reframing process
- use the classic reframing levers explicitly: broaden, narrow, invert, shift actor, shift time horizon, change unit of analysis, change success criterion
- for each restatement, ask 'what assumption does this framing make that the original did not, and vice versa?' — the assumption delta is the analytical payoff
- the goal is not to find a 'correct' framing but to make the choice of framing a conscious, reasoned decision rather than an inherited default
